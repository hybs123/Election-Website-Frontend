Details about candidates participating in Election 2024

Bhartiya Janta Party (BJP)

With a foundation in the Bharatiya Jana Sangh since 1951, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) was formally founded in 1980 and has been promoting pro-Hindu values ever since. Even though the 1989 election was successful, criticism followed the 1992 demolition of the Babri Mosque. A brief BJP government came to power in 1996, and Atal Bihari Vajpayee led a majority government in 1998. The reason for Vajpayee's resignation was the 2004 defeat.

Narendra Modi (born September 17, 1950, Vadnagar, India) Indian politician and government official who rose to become a senior leader of the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP). In 2014 he led his party to victory in elections to the Lok Sabha (lower chamber of the Indian parliament), after which he was sworn in as prime minister of India. Prior to that he had served (2001-14) as chief minister (head of government) of Gujarat state in western India.

Following a triumphant campaign in 2014, Narendra Modi—presenting himself as a practical leader—was named prime minister of India. Prioritizing economic changes, his administration relaxed restrictions on foreign investment and concentrated on improving transportation infrastructure. By welcoming Chinese President Xi Jinping and holding a fruitful meeting with US President Barack Obama, Modi accomplished diplomatic victories. While serving as prime minister, Modi advanced Hindu culture and carried out important economic reforms, like as the Goods and Services Tax (GST) and demonetization. The reforms broadened the tax base even as GDP growth slowed and unemployment rose, falling short of the public's hopes for economic expansion.

Indian National Congress (INC)

The Indian National Congress was established in 1885 and under the leadership of Mahatma Gandhi and Bal Gangadhar Tilak changed from being a moderate reform party to a force for independence. It was crucial to the independence of India, with Jawaharlal Nehru ruling from 1951 to 1964. Despite having dominated history, support began to decline in the latter half of the 20th century, and it finally made a comeback in 2004.

Rahul Gandhi (born June 19, 1970, Delhi, India) Indian politician and leading figure of the Indian National Congress (Congress Party). He was born the son of Rajiv Gandhi, a grandson of Indira Gandhi, and a great grandson of Jawaharlal Nehru, all of whom served as prime ministers of India. His mother, Sonia Gandhi, also played a leading role in the Congress Party.!

The Nehru-Gandhi dynasty's scion Rahul Gandhi joined the Lok Sabha in 2004 as a representative of the Congress Party. He advanced to become the party's vice president in 2013 and briefly led the Congress Party starting in late 2017, despite criticism for his alleged elitism. Even while his overt demonstration of Hindu devotion drew criticism, it helped the party win the 2018 state elections. Gandhi resigned as the head of the party in 2019, even though the Lok Sabha elections showed some improvement. He had legal troubles in 2023 and was found guilty of slander, which resulted in his removal from the Indian Parliament.

Trinamool Congress (TMC)

Formerly a member of the national ruling coalition of the Congress Party, the All India Trinamool Congress (AITC) became an independent entity. A major change in the political scene of West Bengal was brought about by Mamata Banerjee's AITC, which led to her being the first female chief minister of the state.

Indian politician Mamata Banerjee is a groundbreaking individual who was born in Kolkata, India on January 5, 1955. As the first female chief minister of West Bengal since 2011, she overcame early personal obstacles to rise from a lower middle class background. Having earned several degrees, Banerjee began politics while still in college and went on to win several elections. Most notably, she represented Kolkata in the Lok Sabha from 1984 to 2009.

Prominent Indian politician Mamata Banerjee served in important administrative capacities in the federal government, most notably as the Minister of Railways. She created the All India Trinamool Congress (AITC) in 1997 to oppose the incumbent Communist Party while retaining close ties to West Bengal. Banerjee earned support in 2006 after spearheading a hunger strike against forced land acquisition, despite early defeats. After three decades of communist administration, AITC's revival resulted in victory in the 2011 state elections. As the first female chief minister of West Bengal, Banerjee, sometimes known as "Didi," ran on a platform of human rights and women's safety.

Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK)

After splitting from the Dravidian Federation, C.N. Annadurai created the Dravidian Progressive Federation (DMK) in 1949, drawing inspiration from E.V. Ramaswami Naicker's pro-Tamil campaigning. After initially supporting separation, the DMK changed its emphasis to Tamil Nadu's Dravidian population and resisted Hindi imposition. It rose to prominence in 1967 after Annadurai was appointed chief minister and it won the state elections. Following the passing of Annadurai in 1971, Karunanidhi carried on the success.

Muthuvel Karunanidhi Stalin born 1 March 1953 is an Indian politician serving as the 8th and current Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu since 2021. The son of the former Chief Minister M. Karunanidhi, Stalin has been the president of the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK) party since 28 August 2018. He served as the 45th Mayor of Chennai from 1996 to 2002 and the 1st Deputy Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu from 2009 to 2011.Stalin was placed 24th on the list of India's Most Powerful Personalities in 2022 by The Indian Express.

M.K. Stalin started his political career in his early teens when he founded the DMK Gopalapuram Youth Wing. As a devoted activist, he participated in the 1967 elections as a 14-year-old voter for his uncle, Murasoli Maran. Stalin's dedication resulted in his election to the DMK General Committee in 1973. Notably, he became well-known across the country in 1976 after being brutally incarcerated during the Emergency and losing a DMK prisoner. He passed his final year BA exams while incarcerated in spite of hardship. Stalin's unwavering commitment to hard work paid off, as he was elevated to the post of DMK youth wing secretary in 1982 and remained there for more than 40 years.

Aam Aadmi Party (AAP)

When the Aam Aadmi Party (AAP) was founded on October 2, 2012, Indian politics underwent a sea change. It broke down barriers and embraced people from all walks of life, launching the first form of crowdsourced election fundraising. Dedicated to eliminating corruption and offering a substitute political framework, the AAP made its electoral debut in the Delhi Assembly election held in December 2013.

Born on August 16, 1968, Arvind Kejriwal is Delhi's seventh chief minister. He was first elected in December 2013 and held the position until February 2014 when he was re-elected. He was a career bureaucrat who established the Aam Aadmi Party (AAP) in 2012 and led the party to an unprecedented majority in the Delhi Assembly elections of 2015. A laureate of the Ramon Magsaysay Award for his anti-corruption efforts, Kejriwal resigned in 2014 due to legal difficulties. His commitment to political leadership and grassroots action has had a big influence on Indian politics.

Born on August 16, 1968, Arvind Kejriwal has been Delhi's seventh chief minister since February 2015. He has held the position from December 2013 to February 2014. Formerly employed as a bureaucrat, he established the Aam Aadmi Party (AAP) in 2012 and went on to win an unprecedented 67 assembly seats out of 70 in the 2015 Delhi Assembly elections. A laureate of the Ramon Magsaysay Award for his anti-corruption efforts, Kejriwal resigned in 2014 as a result of legal issues. The Public Cause Research Foundation was established as a result of his commitment. After graduating from IIT Kharagpur with a degree in Mechanical Engineering, he left the Indian Revenue Service to change the political scene of Delhi.

Shiv Sena (SS)

Bal Thackeray (born January 23, 1926, Pune, Maharashtra state, India—died November 17, 2012, Mumbai) Indian journalist and politician, founder of the Shiv Sena (“Army of Shiva”) political party, and advocate of a strong pro-Hindu policy in India. Under his leadership the Shiv Sena became a dominant political force in the western Indian state of Maharashtra.

Born on July 27, 1960, Uddhav Balasaheb Thackeray led Maharashtra as its 19th chief minister from 2019 to 2022. As Leader of the House during this time, he oversaw the Maharashtra Legislative Assembly. As president of the Maha Vikas Aghadi since 2019 and the Shiv Sena (Uddhav Balasaheb Thackeray) since 2022, Uddhav Thackeray is currently in office. Prior to joining the Maharashtra Legislative Council in 2020, he served in important capacities for the Shiv Sena as a working president and leader.

Uddhav Thackeray began his political career in 2002 as the campaign manager for the Shiv Sena in the Brihan Mumbai Municipal Corporation elections. After being named working president in 2003, he became Saamana's chief editor in 2006 and remained there till his retirement in 2019 to become chief minister of Maharashtra. After parting ways with his cousin Raj Thackeray in 2006, Uddhav guided Shiv Sena and was elected president of the organisation in 2013. 2019 saw Shiv Sena leave the NDA in order to create Maha Vikas Aghadi with the UPA and win the Maharashtra government. Thackeray, who disapproved of the BJP's coalition strategy, said that he was leaving the NDA in 2022 because of betrayal.

YSR Congress Party (YSR)

The Yuvajana Sramika Rythu Congress Party (YSRCP or YCP) is a regional political party in Andhra Pradesh, India, focused on youth, labor, and farmers. Led by Y. S. Jagan Mohan Reddy, who also serves as the state's chief minister, it currently holds 20 seats, making it the fifth-largest party in the Lok Sabha.The YSRCP is currently well-represented in state and national assemblies of the government.

Chief Minister YS Jagan Mohan Reddy has made it a priority to implement election pledges since taking office on May 30, 2019, with a particular emphasis on women's welfare through Direct Benefit Transfer and the awarding of house site pattas in their names. Significant success was made as a result of government initiatives such as direct benefit transfer, favorable industry policies, and a strong village and ward secretariat structure. A notable emphasis on women's social, economic, and political inclusion was placed by the YS Jagan Mohan Reddy government through programs including Amma Vodi, Vidya Devena, Vasati Devena, Cheyutha, Asara, house site pattas, and reserved posts.

The Yuvajana Sramika Rythu Congress Party (YSRCP), which was founded in 2011, gained prominence after capturing the majority of seats that were left vacant in ensuing by-elections. It won fifteen assembly seats and the Nellore Lok Sabha seat in 2012. Despite losing the Andhra Pradesh Legislative Assembly election in 2014, the YSRCP made a comeback in 2019 and is now India's fifth-largest political party. It won 151 out of 175 seats in the 2019 Andhra Pradesh Legislative Assembly election, led by Y. S. Jagan Mohan Reddy. The YSRCP is currently well-represented in state and national assemblies of the government.

Historical Timeline of Indian Constitution

Dr. Ambedkar Filing the Constitution of India in 1946

The Constituent Assembly, chosen by members of provincial assemblies, drafted the Indian Constitution. With 389 members (down to 299 after the partition), the assembly worked on its drafting for over three years, covering 165 days and eleven sessions. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar played a substantial influence, as T.T. Krishnamachari acknowledged, however the Drafting Committee had difficulties because of vacancies. Ambedkar acknowledged Sir B.N. Rau, the Constitutional Advisor, for a preliminary draft. Ambedkar acknowledged group efforts on November 25, 1949, at his farewell speech. After discussing 7,635 modifications, which addressed 2,473 in the updated draft constitution, the assembly represented a cooperative process that resulted in the formation of this historic text.

Indirectly elected representatives in the Constituent assembly of india 1946-49

After gaining independence in 1947, the Constituent Assembly of India was established, and it spent around three years drafting the constitution of the nation. Muslims and Sikhs were granted special representation, and it was not chosen by adult universal suffrage. The Assembly represented a wide range of viewpoints, from extreme Marxists and Hindu revivalists to conservative industrialists, although being predominately drawn from the Congress Party. The Assembly met from December 9, 1946, to January 24, 1950. Jawaharlal Nehru stated that the Assembly's main objective was to deal with India's urgent concerns, such as hunger and poverty, and that these issues needed to be resolved quickly in order for the constitutional efforts to have any significance.

Pt. Jawaharlal Nehru becoming PM by First Lok Sabha Elections in 1952-57

A pivotal event following independence was the October 25, 1951, to February 21, 1952 holding of India's first general elections. The Representation of the People Act delineated the electoral process and was initiated in 1950 under the newly constituted Election Commission. The first Chief Election Commissioner was Sukumar Sen. In accordance with the 1949 Indian Constitution, 1,949 people ran for 489 Lok Sabha seats in the elections. It was the largest election of its time, with over 173 million eligible voters and a 45.7% turnout. With the overwhelming victory of the Indian National Congress, which garnered 364 seats, Jawaharlal Nehru became the country's first democratically elected prime minister.

Pt. Jawaharlal Nehru becoming PM by Second Lok Sabha Elections in 1957-62

India's second general elections occurred from February 24 to June 9, 1957, including polls for state legislatures. Led by Jawaharlal Nehru, the Indian National Congress (INC) secured a second term with a resounding victory, claiming 371 out of 494 Lok Sabha seats. The INC increased both seats and vote share, garnering 48%. The Communist Party emerged as the second-largest party, while independents secured 19% of votes and 42 seats. The INC's dominance reflected Nehru's continued popularity and marked a pivotal phase in post-independence Indian politics.

Gulzari lal Nanda was made the interim PM by third Lok Sabha Elections in 1962-67

With 361 members out of 494 seats, the Indian National Congress (INC) won a sizable majority in the 1957 general elections. Even though the INC's vote share dropped from 48% to 45%, it was still the overwhelming force. Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru served in that capacity till his passing in 1964. Subsequently, Gulzarilal Nanda assumed the role of acting prime minister until Lal Bahadur Shastri, who remained in that position for 19 months until his death. Following Nehru's tenure, Indira Gandhi took over the position in 1966, signaling a change in Indian leadership.

Indira Gandhi becoming PM by Fourth Lok Sabha Elections in 1967-70

With around 250 million voters casting ballots, Indira Gandhi's Congress party won 283 of 520 seats, guaranteeing her party's fourth term in office. The Congress's vote share dropped to about 41% even after winning. Interestingly, six other parties secured ten or more seats; the largest opposition party was C. Rajagopalachari's Swatantra Party, which secured 44 seats. As the electoral landscape changed, Indira Gandhi cemented her political position by assuming the prime ministership twice.

Indira Gandhi becoming PM by Fifth Lok Sabha Elections in 1971-77

After Indira Gandhi broke away from the Congress, her party won an astounding 352 seats out of 518 in the first election, while the Morarji Desai fraction only managed 16 seats. For the third time, Indira Gandhi took on the position of prime minister. During the time that began with her triumph in 1975, India was placed under a state of emergency, which had a profound impact on the political climate of the nation in the years that followed. Indian politics were significantly impacted by the emergency phase, which had a long-lasting effect on the country's political development.

Morarji Desai of Janata Party becoming PM by Sixth Lok Sabha Elections in 1977-79

The Bharatiya Lok Dal (BLD), which subsequently became a component of the Janata Party, earned a historic victory in the post-emergency elections, which was the first time the Congress was beaten. The BLD, which was founded in 1974 by uniting seven groups that opposed Indira Gandhi's dictatorial leadership, teamed up with the Jan Sangh and Indian National Congress (Organization) to run as the Janata Party in the 1977 elections. The Janata Party established India's first non-Congress administration by winning 295 of 542 seats. After the Janata alliance parties withdrew their support, Morarji Desai was appointed prime minister and was succeeded in 1979 by Charan Singh.

Indira Gandhi becoming PM by Seventh Lok Sabha Elections in 1980-84

After the Janata experiment failed, Indira Gandhi's Congress (I) triumphantly reclaimed power by winning 353 of the 529 seats that were up for grabs. The parties from the erstwhile Janata coalition were unable to repeat their prior achievements. Notably, during this time there was no designated Leader of Opposition (LoP). After the difficulties of the post-emergency and Janata Party eras, Indira Gandhi's decisive win signaled a dramatic political turnabout for Congress (I).

Rajiv Gandhi becoming PM by Eighth Lok Sabha Elections in 1984-89

Anti-Sikh riots broke out in India after Indira Gandhi was killed in 1984, and Sikhs were targeted in pogroms in retaliation. The Congress party, led by Rajiv Gandhi, the son of Indira, took advantage of the sympathy wave to win 404 out of 514 seats, securing a landslide victory. In its first elections, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) won two seats in Andhra Pradesh (now Telangana) and Gujarat. Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi took over the position. The terrible circumstances surrounding Indira Gandhi's murder and the ensuing anti-Sikh riots overshadowed the election.

Vishwanath Pratap Singh of National Front with the support of BJP becoming PM by Ninth Lok Sabha Elections in 1989-91

The Congress suffered setbacks amid the Bofors scandal, LTTE concerns, and other issues. A hung parliament—the first in Indian history—was the outcome of the election, with no party winning a majority. Out of 529 seats, the Congress won 197, the Janata Dal won 143, and the BJP won 85. The BJP achieved notable victories. With the backing of the BJP and left parties, the Janata Dal led the National Front administration. The prime minister is now Vishwanath Pratap Singh, also known as VP Singh. In 1990, Chandra Shekhar broke away from the party due to internal issues and founded the Samajwadi Janata Party. With the help of the Congress, Chandra Shekhar took over after Vice President Singh resigned. However, this arrangement was not long-lasting, since new elections were held within two years.

PV Narsimha Rao of UPA becoming PM by tenth Lok Sabha Elections in 1991-96

The Ram Janmabhoomi-Babri Masjid controversy and the OBC reservation proposed by the Mandal Commission were the two main causes of Rajiv Gandhi's assassination during the 1991 general elections, sometimes referred to as the "Mandal-Mandir" elections. OBCs were given a 27% reservation in government posts by the Mandal Commission, which was put into place by the VP Singh administration. The contentious Babri Masjid in Ayodhya was at the center of the Mandir controversy. Following the riots, there was division along caste and religious lines. There was no majority party; the Congress got 232 seats and the BJP 120 out of 521. After forming a minority administration led by P V Narasimha Rao, Manmohan Singh was chosen to lead the economy and eventually become prime minister.

HD Deve gowda becoming PM by Eleventh Lok Sabha Elections in 1996-98

In a hung parliament following the 1996 elections, the BJP secured 161 seats, the Congress 140, and the Janata Dal 46. 129 seats were claimed by regional parties, such as the DMK, TDP, and Shivsena. As per custom, the President extended an invitation to the BJP to establish a coalition administration. But after 13 days, Atal Bihari Vajpayee announced his resignation as prime minister. Congress gave the Janata Dal-led "United Front" outside backing but declined to form the government. After taking office unannounced, H. D. Deve Gowda led the country for eighteen months before resigning. I. K. Gujral briefly replaced him but faced opposition from the Janata Dal.

Atal Bihari Vajpayee from NDA becoming PM by Twelfth Lok Sabha Elections in 1998-99

Amidst the Kargil War, the 1999 elections yielded a resultant BJP of 182 seats, Congress of 114, and regional parties of 158 out of 543 seats. This time, the BJP brought together a more solid National Democratic Alliance (NDA), which became the first non-Congress alliance to serve out a complete five-year tenure. For the third time, Atal Bihari Vajpayee assumed the role of prime minister. Following the Kargil battle, there was a surge in nationalistic feelings leading up to the election, which helped the BJP win a majority and build a strong coalition government.

Atal Bihari Vajpayee from NDA becoming PM by Thirteenth Lok Sabha Elections in 1999-2004

The Kargil conflict was a backdrop for the 1999 elections. With 182 seats won, the BJP became the largest party, while the Congress garnered 114 seats. Parties from the region secured 158 seats. The National Democratic partnership (NDA), which the BJP created, is now more stable and is the first non-Congress partnership to serve out a full five-year tenure. For the third time, Atal Bihari Vajpayee assumed the role of prime minister. Key events that shaped the political landscape during the term of the BJP-led NDA administration included the Kargil conflict, the nuclear testing at Pokhran, and the historic Lahore Summit with Pakistan.

Manmohan Singh from UPA becoming PM by Fourteenth Lok Sabha Elections in 2004-09

In the 2004 elections, the BJP, promoting the 'India Shining' campaign, aimed for early polls. While securing the middle-class vote, the Congress and regional parties gained support from the poorer sections, leading to the NDA's defeat. The BJP won 138 seats, Congress improved to 145, and regional parties secured 159. Conceding defeat, the BJP allowed the Congress to form the United Progressive Alliance (UPA) with external support from the left parties. Amidst controversy over her foreign origin, Sonia Gandhi declined the Prime Ministership, leading to Manmohan Singh's appointment as the Prime Minister.

Manmohan Singh from UPA becoming PM by Fifteenth Lok Sabha Elections in 2009-14

With the passage of laws like the Right to Information (RTI) and the National Rural Employment Guarantee Scheme (NREGS), which included the 2008 farm loan waiver, the Congress-led UPA delivered on its electoral pledges in 2009. The UPA, led by Manmohan Singh, was up against the L.K. Advani-led NDA before the polls. Compared to 2004, the Congress won 206 seats, a considerable increase, while the BJP got just 116. 146 seats were claimed by regional parties. With this win, the UPA was sworn in for a second consecutive term as prime minister, with Manmohan Singh taking the oath of office.

Narendra Modi of BJP becoming PM by Sixteenth Lok Sabha Elections in 2014-19

Corruption claims and controversies, including those involving 2G, Coal Block, Adarsh, and the Commonwealth Games, dogged the UPA's second term. The situation was made worse by Prime Minister Manmohan Singh's perceived lack of authority and his quiet. Rahul Gandhi was successfully eclipsed by the BJP's portrayal of Narendra Modi as their prime ministerial candidate. With 282 seats, the BJP easily won a majority in the 2014 elections, while the Congress had its worst-ever showing with just 44 seats. This was the first time a party had achieved a majority on its own since 1984.

Narendra Modi of BJP becoming PM by Seventeenth Lok Sabha Elections in 2019-24

The BJP gained a larger mandate by riding the wave of nationalism, popular policies, and the lack of a rival leader to Narendra Modi. With the support of the NDA, the BJP led by Narendra Modi crossed the 350-seat threshold after winning 303 seats on its own. Only Jawaharlal Nehru and Indira Gandhi had ever secured a single-party majority in India's history until Modi's remarkable achievement of doing so for a second term in a row. With just 52 seats won, including a loss in Amethi for Congress President Rahul Gandhi, the Congress suffered yet another setback.

Government Initiatives

Pradhan mantri jan Dhan Yojana

As many as 228.1 million new bank accounts have been opened as on August 3, 2016, an increase of 31% from 174 million in 2015-24% of these accounts (“zero-balance accounts”, in officialese) had no money, a reduction of 22% from 2015. The balance in the accounts increased 85% over one year, from Rs 22,033 crore to Rs 40,795 crore. The previous United Progressive Alliance (UPA) government had opened more than 50 million “no-frills” accounts-as they called them-for the poor over five years, but most were never used, IndiaSpend reported in October 2014.

Swachh Bharat Abhiyan

A nationwide FactChecker check revealed the claim that 100% of India’s schools was not true. Random checks across seven states revealed widespread infirmities, such as: Many schools, from urban New Delhi to backward and often remote areas such as Chatra district (Jharkhand) and Sedam Taluka, Gulbarga district (Karnataka), did not have toilets. The specific claim that every school now has separate toilets for boys and girls in all schools was not true.

Give It Up Abhiyan

As many as 10.4 million people gave up their LPG subsidy voluntarily, according to recent official data from the ministry of oil and petroleum. The Centre launched Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana to provide LPG connections to 50 million women from families who live below the poverty line over a period of three years, starting from financial year 2016-17.

Electricity to Villages

As many as 587,569 of 597,464 villages (98.1%) in India were “electrified” as on June 30, 2016, which means only 9,895 do not have electricity, according to a recent report by the Central Electricity Authority (CEA).In Uttar Pradesh, for instance, three of four households get electricity for less than 12 hours a day. In Jharkhand, only 2% of electrified households get electricity for 20 or more hours; 81% do not get four or more hours in the evenings, while 60% face three or more days of total blackouts every month, IndiaSpend reported in October 2015.
